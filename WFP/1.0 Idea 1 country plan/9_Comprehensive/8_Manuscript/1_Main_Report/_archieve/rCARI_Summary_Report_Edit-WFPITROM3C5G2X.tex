\section{Introduction}

The overall goal of this study was to assess the validity of the rCARI module(s) against conventional CARI modules for measuring food security within the same population. The study compares indicators collected through phone surveys with conventional face-to-face survey, considering the latter as the standard standard methodology/gold standard. The following food security indicators were compared: Food Consumption Score (FCS), reduced Coping Strategy Index (rCSI), Livelihood Coping Strategy (LCS), Food Expenditure Share (FES), and income sources and income change. The Economic Capacity to Meet Essetial Needs (ECMEN) indicator which is sometimes used to aggregate CARI will not be tested in this study, as it requires a standard long expenditure module which cannot be collected over the phone. In addition, ECMEN calculation requires having an updated value of the minimum expenditure basket (MEB) of the study population, which is not available for the study areas. In its stead, a shortened version of the standard expenditure module will be used to test the possibility of collecting FES data over the phone.

Studying the validity of using the phone to collect food security indicators is a key step in judging the appropriateness of the rCARI methodology to report food insecurity prevalence. However, Testing the comparability of these indicators through different modalities of data collection (Face-to-Face (F2F) vs. Phone) should be read within the wider consideration around phone survey modality of data collection. For example, other factors aside the modules should be considered while reading results of any remote survey.

–	First, mobile phone ownership (mobile phone penetration rate in the study area) could likely exclude the most vulnerable population from the sample. 

–	Second, mobile phone network coverage; Even in countries with high penetration rates, network coverage could be a problem, especially when electricity cuts are frequent and local infrastructure, such as communication masts, are not evenly distributed (World Bank, 2020).

–	Third, response rate: It is well documented that the response rate in phone surveys is usually lower than in face-to-face surveys (L’Engle et al., 2018; World Bank, 2020). Response rate is usually lower among the well-off population which could contribute to sample skewness.
 
These factors could may lead to skewed sample from phone surveys especially of the goal to report national representative results/outcomes. Nevertheless, there are several statistical weighting techniques that can adjust the results of phone surveys based baseline variables like using demographics characteristics of the most recent face-to-face surveys (Nagpal et al. 2021; Glazerman et al. 2020; Valliant et al. 2013; Elkasabi, 2015). This allows the final results of such exercises to be aggregated and extrapolated to reflect some level of representativeness. However, this current study remains insulated from these confounding issues since the main idea is not to report results at the national level but rather to compare how the two methods perform within the same population with similar characteristics. For this reason, the study does not focus on weighting techniques to adjust for sampling skewness and response rate. Nonetheless, the study design adopted in Central African Republic (CAR), could allow for testing of sample skewness due to mobile phone ownership and coverage (see CAR sample design in Annex III). CAR has one of the lowest mobile phone penetration rates globally (approximately 70\% of the population do not own/use mobile phones).

\section{Study design}

The study was implemented in a randomized controlled setup following all the requirements of a Randomized Control Trial (RCT). However, for the current study, we call the study methodology a randomized control study not confused readers with the RCT as used in the medical field. 

Food security data was collected from two statistically comparable groups of households within the same sample. Standardized remote and face-to-face questionnaires were administered to the sampled households randomized to receive either of two modalities: remote or face-to-face. The study is being implemented across six countries: two in Africa, two Latin America, one in Southeast Asia, and one in the Middle East.

After the first round of data collection covering the period March 2023 to August 2023, the study has been implemented in 3 study countries; Myanmar, Ecuador and CAR interviewing a total of 3,755 households. The remaining 3 countries are planned to start soon.

	\subsection{Selection of study areas and participants}

The study areas (Admin1 and Admin5 areas) were randomly selected in collaboration with WFP CO staff based on a common criteria of food insecurity prevalence, mobile network and accessibility.
After taking the informed consent from potential participants/respondents, they were enlisted to participated in the study. Listing of potential households/respondents was done in each Admin5 area through a random walk in the community and selecting every 3rd house. A minimum of 100 potential participating households were listed per Admin5 area (except for Myanmar where the study was conducted amongst 20 IDP camps and some of the camps had fewer than 100 households) and this served as the sampling frame for randomization process. The initial sample size was 1665 households in Myanmar, 2372 households in Ecuador, and 2500 households in CAR. 

In the listing phase of the study, baseline variables that included demographic characteristics of potential respondents and their households was collected in Ecuador and CAR. This was not possible in Myanmar because of some restrictions by the local authorities. Listed households were then randomly assigned to receive one of two modalities; half of the sample were assigned to receive remote/phone interviews (called the pseudo-treatment group) and the other half assigned to receive face-to-face interviews (called the control group). The study targeted a minimum of 600 completed interviews for each modality.

	\subsection{Myanmar}
	
The study in Myanmar was implemented Kachin and Shan regions. The sampling list was compiled using WFP beneficiaries made up of internally displaced persons (IDPs) in the two regions. The would allow to understand how the instruments perform among different groups of respondents. A total of 20 camps were selected as the primary sampling units (PSU), respondents/households were then randomly selected for the RCT stage. 

Data collection was conducted by WFP field staff after a 3-day training that was lead by the regional Bureau Bangkok in collaboration with Myanmar CO and supported remotely by the rCARI team in HQ. Training and actual data collection was conducted in Myanmar.

A total of 610 (73\%) household interviews were completed for the face-to-face modality, while 483 (58\%) households were successfully interviewed in the phone/remote modality. A balancing text for the validity of the randomization show that the baseline respondent/household characteristics is well balanced between the two groups (modalities). Only differed in terms of the respondent age and number of income sources reported by the household.

	\subsection{Ecuador}

In Ecuador, the study in was implemented in the Amazonia, Coasta and Sierra regions (3 Admin1 areas). These regions were selected based on accessibility, food security prevalence and security consideration for the team given the political upheaval at the time of implementing the study.  A total of 27 Admin5 areas (villages) were randomly selected from these 3 Admin1 areas and visited in two phases. 

A total of 2,374 households details were enlisted into the study after receiving the written consent. Enumerator training was  jointly conducted by the Ecuador CO and RBP colleagues in Spanish over a 4-day period with a one day simulation each for remote and F2F instruments. Both interviews were conducted in Spanish the official language of the Ecuador. 

	
	\subsection{Central African Republic}
	
The study in CAR was implemented in 2 Admin1 (Prefectures) - Mbomou and Nana-Gribizi the two most accessible areas (aside Bangui) that also has a mix of different food insecure groups.The secondary sampling of PSUs followed similar pattern using field access (security situation) and mobile phone network coverage. A total of 25 Admin5 areas (villages) were randomly selected from the list of Admin5 areas compiled jointly be the CAR CO and the local mobile phone service providers (to ensure that all Admin5 areas selected had mobile phone network). Admin5 areas without phone coverage were automatically excluded from the sampling frame. 
To ensure that the sample was a mix of both rural and urban households (different food security situations between these areas),  sub layer of locality was included during the selection of Admin5 areas so that the final sampled villages was made up of rural and urban areas. Implementation of the household listing and survey was led by ICASEES, the national statistical office of CAR.  

The listing of households for the study sample was conducted through the random walk procedure in each village enlisting a minimum of 100 households per Admin5. A total of 2500 households/respondents were listed to participate in the study. Drawing from the experience and data of Myanmar and Ecuador, a number of demographic characteristics such as age of household head, gender of household, household size, and ownership of common household assets was collected during household listing.
 
Training of enumerators was conducted by in person facilitators from HQ with the support from CAR CO. The training was conducted in French over a in 4-day period with one additional day used for field testing, adjusting and adapting the final questionnaires (F2F \& Remote) before deployment for data collection. Though enumerator training was done in French, deployment for the actual field work was done systematically by the CO and ICASEES so that each enumerator was sent to localities where they could speak the local dialects in case a respondent does not speak French thus require the interview is conducted in the local dialect.

CAR as mentioned has a large proportion of the population without mobile phones (estimated 70\% of the total populace). To ensure that the study compares appropriate households in terms of phone ownership and coverage, a slightly different sampling and randomization methods were applied in the CAR (see Figure 2 for all the sampling and randomization stages adopted in CAR). This allowed a segregation of phone-owners and non-phone-owners so that results are not only compared over modality but also on the phone-ownership variable. A statistical test of balance between the different groups show that respondents are significantly balanced (see Annex for details).

	\subsection{Overall study implementation}

Data collection for both modalities was done simultaneously to cover the same period of time. The fieldwork was originally planned over a 3 week period (covering 1 week for enumerator training, adjusting questionnaire and 2 weeks for actual household interviews) in each pilot country. \section{Challenges of the study by country}

In this section, the main challenges faced in the 3 pilot countries are listed. 

\subsection{Myanmar}

1.	Outdated listing data
2.	Poor/unstable network coverage
3.	Mobility of respondents/households
4.	Absence of baseline data (for assessing the attrition characteristics) 
5.	Limited coverage of the study population (covered only IDPs)
6.	Typhoon season affected the implementation period.
7.	Restricted access to the field
8.	Distrust from respondents


\subsection{Ecuador}

1.	Issues of security for enumerators in some communities.
		a.	Parts of the country were not accessible due to safety concerns for the face-to-face enumerators.
2.	Misunderstanding with the local authorities preventing field access by F2F team.
		a.	Dues to political reasons, some local authorities refused enumerators access leading to dropping of these communities.
		b.	Distrust from local authorities and in some cases respondents.
3.	Invalid respondent of household contact information (collected during the listing phase)
4.	Poor phone coverage in remote areas
5.	Disproportionate ownership of phones between urban and rural areas (low phone ownership in rural areas)
6.	Low response rate particularly among the remote sample
		a.	High attrition between the time of receiving consent and actual interview date.

\subsection{CAR}

1.	Restricted access to the field because of deteriorating security situation
2.	Bad phone network/infrastructure 
		a.	Most parts of the country do not have phone network coverage.
		b.	In places where there is coverage, poor infrastructure and constant electricity cuts affected call quality.
		c.	No access to electricity for charging phones particularly in rural areas.
3.	Low phone ownership in the population
		a.	Only 25\% of households had phones (similarly to the national statistic)
4.	Logistical challenges (Enumerator deployment)
		a.	Depended on the UNHAS to deploy enumerators this affected how quickly deployment could be done (flights to study areas twice a week)
		b.	Acquisition and distribution of phones/SIM cards-Challenges with moving them to the field for distribution.
5.	Low response rate in the remote arm (High Attrition)
6.	Lack of remote data collection platform by the Vendor (ICASEES) 
7.	Weather/rainy season

\section{Summary and conclusions}

Disclaimer: the following conclusions are preliminary based on the data collected from the initial 3 pilot countries and thus should be interpreted with the following caveats. First, the results from CAR are based on a sub-sample of the overall sample (only phone owners’). Also, in Myanmar results of FES and rCARI are based on half the households that received the remote interviews (see Annex I and II for more details). 

From the preliminary analysis of the available data, the following initially conclusions are made:     

rCARI using income \& income change indicator (rCARI-Inc) is not statistically different from the standard/conventional CARI (CARI-FES) when used in Myanmar \& Ecuador. In CAR, where it is significantly different, (rCARI-Inc) tends to underestimate food insecurity by 21\% points. 

Analysing (CARI-Inc) and (CARI-FES) from the same households interviewed through F2F modality revealed that (CARI-Inc) leads to lower food insecurity estimation by 6 to 22 percentage points. 

FCS, rCSI, FES and income sources indicators collected over phone tend to give similar or slightly different results than F2F data collection. Differences in FCS (0 to 8 points), rCSI (-1 to 4 points) FES (5-8 points), and income sources (0 to 8 percentage points in category 4) (1 to 11 percentage points in category 3)

LCS collected over phone tends to produce higher estimation of food insecurity than those collected from face to face. The difference is LCS crisis + emergency coping is (18\% to 28\% points).
 
Regardless of the modality of the collection, the absolute prevalence of the FES indicator (looking at category 3 and 4, i.e., FES > 65\%) is much higher than the absolute prevalence of the income source indicator (looking at irregular income + no income categories). The difference between the income source and FES prevalence coming from the F2F group was 18\% points in Myanmar, 25\% points in Ecuador and 44\% points in CAR. 
 
Although LCS over estimated food insecurity in the three countries by 18\% to 28\% points, the conservative estimate of income source compared to FES (-18\% to -44\% points) could explain the equivalence between CARI standard methodology (CARI-FES) with the (rCARI-Inc) in Myanmar and Ecuador (Table 3). This can explain the underestimation of the food insecurity in CAR that was found to be the most extreme (-44\% points).
