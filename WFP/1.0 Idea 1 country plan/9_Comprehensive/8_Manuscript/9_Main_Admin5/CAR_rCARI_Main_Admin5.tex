\textbf{Summary of the Key Points of the Results}

The results show that; 
\begin{itemize}
	\item Households without mobile phones (those conventionally excluded in CATI surveys) are significantly more food insecure (more than 37\% points) regardless of the CARI calculation methodology used. 
	\item The standard rCARI methodology (income choice and income change variable) tend to significantly underestimates AFI (Acute Food Insecurity) compared to the conventional CARI methodology-driven largely by the income indicator (difference is up to 20\% points).
	\item The replacing the FES indicator with the income source \& income change indicator leads to lower food insecurity numbers (between 16\% and 24\% points).
	\item Households, generally tend to report better food consumption (better FCS) and worse coping (LCS \& rCSI) over the phone than they report in F2F.
	\item The form (questionnaire) length does not have any statistical effect on AFI classification.
	\item Phone ownership has a significant influence on AFI classification (the effect ranges between 14\% to 21\% depending on whether FES or Income indicator is used). 
	\item Survey modality on the other hand is found not have any statistical effect.
\end{itemize}

\section{Regression analysis}

In this section of the analysis, regression analysis is conducted to determine the effect of survey modality and phone ownership in explaining the food security of the household controlling for individual and household level characteristics. The model estimated is specified as follows:

	\[Y_{i}=\beta M_{i}+ \gamma P_{i}+ \delta X_{i}+ \varepsilon_{i} \tag{1}\] 

where \(Y_{i}\) is a binary indicator of the food security status of the \(i^{th}\) household taking the value of one if the respondent is food insecure
and zero otherwise (calculated independently for FES and income indicators respectively), \(X_{i}\) is a vector of respondent's and household level characteristics such as age of respondent, sex, education in years, household size, dependency ratio and presence of persons with disability in the household, \(M_{i}\) is a binary indicator \(M_{i}=0/1\) taking the value of 1 if the mode of interview was F2F and zero if  phone, \(P_{i}\) is a phone ownership indicator of the respondent \(P_{i}=1/0\) takes the value of one if the respondent owned a phone zero otherwise and \(\varepsilon_{i}\) is the error term clustered at ADMIN5 level. \(\beta\) and \(\gamma\) are the coefficients of interest showing the effect of modality and phone ownership  on the \(i^{th}\) household's food insecurity outcome respectively.

Equation (1) is implemented as a simple logistic regression for the whole sample. The results of the regression analysis are presented in Table 14. The regression is implemented stepwise in three different ways based on the number covariates included at a time. First, the base model which includes only the interview modality and household ownership of phone (columns 1 \& 4). The second model includes the base model and the household demographics collected during the listing (columns 2 \& 5). The third and final model includes all the variables while controlling for the form length (columns 3 \& 6).

The results of the base model (columns 1 \& 4) show that owning a phone significantly reduces the probability of being food insecure by 19 percentage points (FES) and 25 percentage points (income choice \& income change) respectively. This effect is however reduced to 14 percentage (FES) and 17 percentage (Income choice \& income change) respectively. 

Modality of interview and length of forms however are found not to have any statistical significance in explaining food insecurity classification.

The analysis is also performed excluding households that received mobile phones from the research team. This level of analysis would represent a typical CATI survey (i.e., only households with access to mobile phones are included in the sampling frame) with the conventional F2F survey sample. The results are included as Table 18 in the Annex. Although the magnitude of effect is different, the results are similar to what has been reported in Table 14.

\section{Summary \& conclusions}
		
The following are some key highlights from the results of the households aggregated at ADMIN5 areas are as follows:

	\begin{itemize}[left=2.5em]
		\item Respondents over the phone generally tend to overreport their food security status compared to F2F interviews. However, the modality doesn’t show significant influence on the final AFI estimation.
		\item The results comparing the standard F2F subsample with the phone owners’ subsample (i.e., a typical CATI sample) show that using the standard CATI in regions/countries/areas where phone penetration is low tends to exclude more vulnerable and food insecure households in so doing underreporting AFI.
		\item The results of the food insecurity aggregation show that, rCARI-Inc methodology significantly underestimates acute food insecurity (AFI) by between 9 and 21 percentage points. This is largely driven by the classification  income sources into the 4 four CARI income groups (e.g. the classification of small scale agricultural households as stable income source category inadvertently puts them in the food secure group  using income while FES classifies same households as being food insecure).
		\item While there is no significant difference between CARI-FES and rCARI-FES in the phone owner subsample, rCARI-Inc significantly underestimates food insecurity by up to 7 percentage points.
		\item The analysis of the long- and short- LCS, FES and FCS modules (F2F) overall does not show any statistically significant difference. This means that by fine-tuning the short modules could potentially replace the longer versions in F2F surveys to reduce survey fatigue both for the interviewee and interviewer.
		\item In terms of the food security indicators,  households tend to  reports higher food consumption (FCS) and worse coping situations (rCSI and LCS)  interviewed over the phone (remote modality) compared to when interviewed F2F. Also, when interviewed remotely, households report significantly higher FES compared to F2F. 
	
	
	\end{itemize}

\subsection{Study limitations \& implementation challenges}
Though the study has been implemented with outmost regard for all theoretical and empirical guidelines and protocols underpinning experimental survey designs, the study nonetheless has some limitations worth mentioning
\begin{itemize}
	\item First, the completed remote sample small. 
	\item Second, although there is no statistical difference between the respondents who received phones from the project and phone owners, their response could be influenced by receipt of the mobile phones. 
	\item Third, the low phone ownership (25\%) and mobile coverage influenced greatly the study implementation, sampling and number of households reached over the phone (remote sample). The response rate was generally low in the remote modality even though phones were distributed to potential respondents.
	\end{itemize}
