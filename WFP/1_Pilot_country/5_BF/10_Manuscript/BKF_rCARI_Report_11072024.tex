	\textbf{Executive Summary}
	
	The Remote Consolidated Approach for Reporting Food Insecurity (rCARI) is an adaptation of the standard CARI methodology, designed specifically for use with data collected through remote modalities. While rCARI retains the core food security indicators of the conventional CARI, it diverges in the measurement of household vulnerability. In place of Food Expenditure Share (FES) or Economic Capacity to Meet Essential Needs (ECMEN)—which are difficult to capture reliably through phone-based surveys—rCARI incorporates indicators related to income source and income change. Although rCARI is currently being piloted in various assessments, it has yet to undergo empirical validation. This study aims to validate the rCARI methodology by comparing its outcomes with those of the standard CARI using data from the same sample populations.

	This study employed a survey experiment using a stratified sample to evaluate and validate the rCARI methodology against the standard CARI methodology. The sampling frame comprised the population from  three purposively selected regions—Plateau Central, Centro-Nord, and Nord Regions (ADMIN1). These regions were chosen based on mobile phone coverage, food security status, and accessibility, considering on-ground security challenges. The study sample was randomly selected from a full household census across 38 villages (ADMIN5) or primary sampling units (PSUs). In total, 4,500 households were drawn randomly (using the random number approach) from the census list of 10,554 households. 

	Sampled households were then randomly assigned to one of two study groups (survey modalities):
	\begin{itemize}
		\item [1.] \textbf{Face-to-Face (F2F) interviews:} Households received standard CARI modules.
		\item [2.] \textbf{Remote (phone) interviews:} Households received rCARI modules.
	\end{itemize}

	In Burkina Faso, challenges such as poor infrastructure and limited accessibility affected field operations. For instance, two ADMIN5 areas were excluded from the study due to security and inaccessible road network. The study ultimately interviewed 2,882 households out of a potential 4,256 households, with 1,863 (65\%) interviewed F2F and 1,019 (35\%) interviewed remotely.

	This report presents the preliminary analysis of data collected between 1–30 September 2024 in Burkina Faso and follows these steps:

	\begin{itemize}
		\item\textbf{Comparison of main food security indicators:} The main food security indicators (Food Consumption Score (FCS), Food Expenditure Score (FES), Reduced Coping Strategies Index (rCSI) and Livelihood Coping Strategies Index (LCSI)) are compared across F2F and remote modalities.
		\item\textbf{Calculation and validation of CARI Versions:}Three variations of CARI are calculated and compared against the standard CARI:
		\begin{itemize}
			\item[1.] CARI with FES that is collected in the remote modality (rCARI-FES).
			\item[2.] CARI with income source \& income change (F2F version), (CARI-Inc).
			\item[3.] CARI with income source \& income change (remote version): This version was developed during the COVID-19 pandemic and is referred to as the standard rCARI in this report (rCARI-Inc).
		\end{itemize}
	\end{itemize}

		
	The results highlight the following key findings:
	 
	\begin{itemize}
		\item In estimating the prevalence of acute food insecurity (AFI) defined as the moderately plus severely food insecure groups, remote CARI using income source and income change indicator reported a significantly lower prevalence (approximately 15 percentage points on average) compared to standard CARI results from F2F modules. This difference was significant across all four CARI categories.
		\item Using the pilot shortened expenditure module implemented in the remote surveys produced results that did not significantly deviate from the standard CARI results in terms of the aggregated AFI.
		\item The results further show that the income choice and income change indicator remained robust, stable and consistent in under stating households' vulnerability regardless of the interview modality (F2F or remote). For example, respondents always underestimated their economic vulnerability when the income source and income change indicator was used by between 14 and 17 percentage points compared to when the full expenditure module (FES) regardless of survey modality.
		\item In general, comparative households tend to report better food security outcome indicators over the phone than those interviewed in F2F.This leads to better remote overall outcome than the F2F.
		\item Regression analysis, controlling for baseline respondent characteristics, showed no statistically significant effect of survey modality on food insecurity group (being classied as food insecure, moderately food insecure and severely food insecure combined), regardless of whether FES or the income source and income change indicator was used. However, the gender of the respondent was significantly associated with the probability of the household being food insecure across both methods (CARI-F2F and rCARI) suggesting potential gender-specific vulnerabilities or reporting biases.
	\end{itemize}
	
		The overarching communication points from the foregoing is that:
	\begin{itemize}[left=2.5em]
		\item \textbf{Survey Modality (F2F vs. Remote):} While challenges such as connectivity and lower response rates may affect remote surveys, when properly implemented they can produce food security assessments outcomes that are comparable to the standard F2F surveys results.
		\item \textbf{Alignment of CARI and rCARI Methodologies:} Compared to FES, the income source and income change indicator as a vulnerability measure tends to under report the severity and prevalence of AFI regardless of the survey modality.
	\end{itemize}

	\section{Introduction}
	
	Food security assessment and reporting are at the core of WFP’s mandate of addressing hunger and food insecurity. Over time, different methodologies have been developed (e.g, IPC, CARI, Cadre Harmonise) and used to assess and report global food insecurity each differing in scope, and purpose. A commonality that cuts across all methods is the use of primary household data collected through face-to-face interviews/surveys. However, in recent times particularly with the outbreak of the Covid-19 pandemic and attendant restrictions on movement and physical contact, where data collection through conventional face-to-face method has been adversely affected. This negatively impacted the ability to assess food security needs because of the data gap created. Developing methodologies to accurately measure and interpret food insecurity especially in emergencies where physical access is restricted thus, is of great importance for WFP and many stakeholders. This is particularly important during emergencies when rapid assessment is required for advocacy and policy to save lives and livelihoods but physical access to the field is restricted either because of health threats or security considerations for enumerators. 
	
	To leverage the increased penetration of technology in operational areas of WFP during the Covid lockdowns, a remote version of the Consolidated Approach for Reporting Food Insecurity (CARI)- the so-called rCARI- was developed as an adaptation of the standard CARI methodology and administered remotely (over the phone). The methodology provided a stop-gap solution during the lockdown, however, questions about the validity of food security numbers it produces remain. For example, how are the remote modules comparable with already standardized face-to-face modules in capturing food insecurity. Although there exist validated studies on the use of face-to-face modules in assessing and reporting on food insecurity, remote methodology such as rCARI remains scant until now. 
	
	The potential advantages of the methodology abound for humanitarian work particularly in hard-to-reach areas where F2F assessment is practicably impossible. For example, the remote methodology has potential to save lives by providing real-time food insecurity data on the exposed population within the shortest possible time to inform policy and advocacy purposes. Furthermore, it can result in better use of scarce resources to achieve high end results by rolling out few targeted modules to assess food insecurity of a larger population.  	
	
	The overall objective of this study is to assess the comparability of the remote consolidated approach for reporting food insecurity - rCARI modules with the conventional CARI modules for assessing and reporting food insecurity. Specifically, the following indicators; Food Consumption Score (FCS), reduced Coping Strategy Index (rCSI), Livelihood Coping Strategy Index (LCSI), Food Expenditure Share (FES), and income sources and income change have been compared over the two modalities. The Economic Capacity to Meet Essential Needs (ECMEN) indicator which is sometimes used capture the economic vulnerability of households when aggregating CARI will not be tested in this study for a number of reasons. First, it requires the standard long expenditure module which cannot be collected over phone. Second, ECMEN calculation requires having an updated value of the minimum expenditure basket (MEB) of the study population, which is not available for the areas where the study is being conducted.
	
	It is important to clarify that the objective of this study is not to report national food security numbers t the national level or aim for external validity across the entire population of each pilot country. Instead, the primary goal is to assess the comparability of the two methodologies: i.e., the standard Consolidated Approach for Reporting Food Insecurity (CARI) with its remote version (rCARI) using a random sample drawn from the same population sample with comparable statistical characteristics. By focusing the study on the same population, it allows for a direct comparison of the effectiveness and accuracy of data collected remotely for reporting household food security. 
	
	The findings of this study is intended to provide valuable insights into how comparable F2F (CARI) and remote (rCARI) modalities (methodologies) are in reporting prevalence of food security. In this regards, conclusions drawn from this study should be interpreted within these caveats and context. Furthermore, while factors such as mobile phone ownership (mobile phone penetration rate), network coverage and survey response rate should be considered when interpreting remote surveys, because these may skew the study sample towards a particular group of respondents. The current study is not affected by these confounding factors for two reasons. First, the objective is not to report results at national level but to assess the effectiveness of remote modules and secondly, the sample used is uniform with comparable observable characteristics making them statistically. By emphasizing internal validity within the selected populations, this study aims to provide robust evidence to inform decision-making on the one side while improving food security assessments particularly in the context of remote data collection.

	The rest of the report is structured as follows. In the next section, the study design and implementation is presented in detail. Section 3 presents the descriptives statistics of the data. Section 4 presents the results of comparative analysis showing how the different module results under the different survey modalities. Section 5 presents the regression results while 6 presents the summary and conclusions. Section outlines next steps including potentially some further analysis.

	\section{Study design \& sample}
		
	The study was implemented as a survey experiment with stratified sample drawn from the same population with comparable statistics. 
	
	Selection of study areas and participating households followed a standard stratified random sampling approach (stratification was done by the different Admin Areas-biggest to smallest but not representative at each admin area) based on 3-point criteria - \textbf{mobile phone coverage, prevalence of food security \& accessibility (considering the security situation on the ground)} - developed and agreed together with the WFP Burkina country office.
	
	The study sample were then randomly selected randomly assigned to one of two groups: F2F or remote groups. The full process outlining the systematic steps (1 - 5) adopted for selecting the different administrative areas and respondent households with annotated explanations are shown in figure 1. 
	
	\begin{figure}[H]
		\begin{center}
			\includegraphics[scale=.65]{2_figure/BF_Sampling.png}
		\end{center}
		\label{fig:Sample_Frame}
		\caption{rCARI Validation Study Sampling Frame}
	\end{figure}
	
	\begin{itemize}[label=--, left=1.5em]
		\item[1.] STEP 1 - Based on the inclusion criteria (phone coverage, prevalence of food security and physical accessibility), three regions were purposively selected for the pilot in Burkina Faso (Plateau Central, Nord \& Centro-Nord Regions). 
		
		\item[2.] STEP 2 - A total of four Provinces (ADMIN2), Ganzourgou, Sanmatenga, Yatenga and Passore, were purposively selected from the regions selected in step 1. The Administrative structure of Burkina Faso allowed for the inclusion of Admin3 areas (communes), therefore four communes were further selected; 2 communes from the Nord region and one each from Centro-Nord and Plateau Central regions respectively. 
		
		\item[3.] STEP 3 - Selection of villages (ADMIN5): A list of villages in the selected communes (that met the inclusion criteria set out in STEP 1) were compiled at this stage to form the primary sampling units (PSUs). A total of 39 PSUs were initially selected (distributed uniformly across the 4 selected communes). The selection of the villages were done taking into consideration food security prevalence situation allowing for a mix of areas  that would represent the different IPC phases in the country as a whole. See Annex Table 18 for the full list of the selected administrative areas selected, population and IPC phases.
		
		\item[4.] STEP 4 - Sampling of households for the study: The sampling frame for study was all households in selected PSUs. A minimum of 100 households per PSU were randomly selected from the census list. Theses selected households were used in the randomization process in the next step.
		
		\item[5.] STEP 5 - Assignment of household to survey modalities: Consented and sampled households from STEP 4 were randomly assigned into one of two survey modalities/groups; F2F (CARI group) and Remote (rCARI group). The experiment was designed such that the sample was randomly assigned equally between the two modalities.
	
		The interviews for both modalities - F2F and remote - were conducted within the same time frame to ensure households were reporting food security situation over the same window and also exposed to the same economic situations or shocks if any. 
	\end{itemize}

For example, Table 9 show that of the households classified as severely food insecure by the income source and income change indicator (n = 175), 83.43\% of these households were also captured and classified as severely food insecure using the FES indicator with the remaining 16.57\% of households being classified as moderately food insecure by the FES. In terms of the moderately food insecure classification, of the households that were classified by the income choice and income change indicator (n = 1,236), 68\% were also classified as moderately food insecure while 32\% were classified as severely food insecure by the FES indicator. 
	
	The results for the higher food security categories on the other hand show significance divergence between the two aggregating methodologies. For example, up to 7 in 10 of all the households are classified as food secure or marginally food secure are classified into a less food secure category by FES with the alignment being found in about 30\% of all classifications in these categories. 
	
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Severe food insecurity is consistently captured by both measures, indicating robust alignment at the lower end of the food security spectrum.
		
		\item CARI using income source and income change indicator may overestimate food security particularly amongst populations with borderline food security, risking exclusion errors in assistance targeting.

	\end{itemize}

	The overarching conclusion of Table 9 shows a strong consistency between income choice and income change and FES based CARI classifications, particularly for severely and moderately food insecure households. This alignment though not perfect provides evidence to support the use of the income based CARI (rCARI) as an effectiveness methodology for identifying vulnerable and food insecure populations. However, in less severe or transitional populations, the two methodologies diverge up to 75\%.

	\section{Regression analysis}
	
	In this section of the analysis, a simple linear regression analysis is conducted to determine the effect of survey modality and phone ownership in explaining the food security (defined as moderately and severely food insecure based on CARI) of the household controlling for individual and household level characteristics. The model estimated is specified as follows:
	
	\[Y_{i}=\beta M_{i}+ \gamma P_{i}+ \delta X_{i}+ \varepsilon_{i} \tag{1}\] 
	
	where \(Y_{i}\) is a binary indicator of the food security status of the \(i^{th}\) household taking the value of one if the respondent is food insecure (made of the moderately food insecure plus severely food insecure CARI categories) and zero otherwise (calculated independently for CARI-FES - Food Insecure FES, and CARI-Inc - Food Insecure Income, indicators respectively), \(X_{i}\) is a vector of respondent's and household level characteristics such as age of respondent, sex, education in years, household size, dependency ratio and presence of persons with disability in the household, \(M_{i}\) is a binary indicator \(M_{i}=0/1\) taking the value of 1 if the mode of interview was F2F and zero if  phone, \(P_{i}\) is phone ownership indicator of the respondent \(P_{i}=1/0\) takes the value of one if the respondent owned a phone zero otherwise and \(\varepsilon_{i}\) is the error term clustered at ADMIN5 level. \(\beta\) and \(\gamma\) are the coefficients of interest showing the effect of modality and phone ownership on the \(i^{th}\) household's food insecurity outcome respectively.
	
	Equation (1) is implemented as a simple logistic regression for the full sample stepwise in three different configuration of the model defined by the number covariates included in each configuration. The results of the analysis are presented in Table 17 below. In the first stage, a base model which includes only the survey modality and gender of the respondent (columns 1 \& 4). The second model includes the base model controlling for additional baseline household demographics collected in the census exercise (columns 2 \& 5). The third and final estimation is the second model plus an interaction term of the sex of the household head and sex of the respondent (columns 3 \& 6).

The inclusion of an interaction term between sex of the respondent and sex of the household did not show any statistical significance. This may reflect:

These put together show that while structural and demographic variables may contribute to food security outcomes, their lack of statistical significance in this model suggests that the sex of the respondent is a strong predictors of food security in Burkina Faso using the FES and income source and income change-based CARI classifications.

	\section{Summary \& conclusions}
	
	In this pilot study, we examined the effect of survey modality (Face-to-Face [F2F] vs. remote) on food security assessment in Burkina Faso. The study employed a survey experiment using a stratified random sample selected from three regions of the country with varying degrees of food insecurity and livelihoods. The experiment was implemented by randomly assigning sampled households to one of the two survey modalities using their baseline observable characteristics as control (Household baseline characteristics were collected during a household census phase that was conducted before the actual survey). The two samples (F2F vs. remote) were then tested for statistical comparability using their their baseline observable characteristics such as age of household head, household size, gender of household head, education of household head, main source of livelihood, dependency ratio, etc.

	In total 2,882 households were interviewed (F2F and remote together), using identical food security assessment modules. Data collection was carried out over a four-week period, with both modalities implemented simultaneously by trained and experienced enumerators. 
	
	The data from this experiment provides important insights for conducting food security assessment using remote methods (phone survey). Below are some key highlights and talking points from the analysis of this pilot study.
	
	\begin{itemize}[left=2.5em]
		\item No significant impact of modality was found at the aggregate level for the food security prevalence reported across the two samples after controlling for respondent and household characteristics. This may indicate that difference arising from the results of the rCARI and CARI aggregation methodologies is driven by the individual indicators like the LCS and income change.
		
		\item Respondents in the remote sample generally tend to report better aggregated food security outcome (report better food security outcomes) than the F2F sample. Intra-household comparison of the two aggregation methodologies (CARI and rCARI) show that households would overstate their economic vulnerability using expenditure while under-reporting using the income choice and income change indicator. This result is consistent in the inter-household analysis of the responses for the two indicators.
		
		\item The Food Expenditure Share (FES) indicator is found to be stable across modalities at the aggregate level though this difference emerge when the comparison is done acros the 4-CARI FES categories.
		
		\item The analysis of the long- and short- LCSI, FES and FCS overall does not show any statistically significant difference. This means that by fine-tuning and increased training, the short modules could potentially replace the longer versions in F2F surveys to reduce survey fatigue both for the interviewee and interviewer. For example the shortened FES module piloted in the phone surveys produced results that were statistically comparable to the standard (long module) FES module that was implemented in in the F2F survey. 
		 
		\item Using the income source and income change indicator may be effective for capturing short-term economic shocks and acute vulnerabilities that do not necessarily reflect the long term consumption patterns and therefore especially valuable for assessment during crises like natural disasters or economic downturns, where income changes directly impact food security.
		\item Further analysis is needed to understand the dynamics of marginally and moderately food-secure households, where the CARI classification are significantly different for the two aggregation methods, i.e., CARI\_FES and CARI\_Income.
.
		\item In terms of the food security indicators, households tend to reports slightly higher food consumption (FCS) and better livelihood coping situations over the phone (remote modality) than when interviewed F2F. 
		%\item CARI\_FES: This probably best suited for long-term monitoring and identifying food insecurity prevalence. Thus, programs focusing on sustained support, such as livelihood improvement and food assistance, should rely on FES data.
	\end{itemize}
	
	The key communication points from the foregoing is that:
	\begin{itemize}[left=2.5em]
		\item \textbf{Survey Modality (F2F vs. Remote):} While challenges such as connectivity issues and lower response rates may not favor remote surveys, when properly implemented, remote surveys can produce food security assessments that are comparable to F2F surveys results. 
		\item \textbf{Alignment of CARI and rCARI Methodologies:} CARI using the income-based indicator as a measure of economic vulnerability of the population tends to adopt a conservative approach in estimating acute food insecurity compared to the use of the FES regardless of the survey modality. Thus the main difference is not in the modality but in the income source and income change indicator which needs further study to align.
		%\item \textbf{Gender \& Income Indicator:} The sensitivity of the respondent's gender to the income choice and income change indicator highlight the importance of adopting nuanced and context-specific approaches to food security measurement.
	\end{itemize}
	
	
	\subsection{Recommendations}
	Based on the foregoing findings from the Burkina Faso study data, the following recommendations are deduced:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item The use of rCARI with the income source and income change indicator leads to under estimating the prevalence of AFI and thus in its current state if used results should be adjusted by on average 15 percentage points to align with CARI.
		\item More refinement of the income source and income change indicator should be pursued at a more global level to better categorize the different income sources into the 4-point CARI categories that can better reflect their economic vulnerability in remote surveys.
		\item Integrate and complement both Food Expenditure Share (FES) and the Income Choice \& Income Change indicators in reporting CARI (CARI-FES and CARI-Inc): The use of both FES and the income source and income change indicators in tandem will capture both long-term trends and short-term vulnerabilities in food security assessments, particularly during crises.
		\item Address gender-specific vulnerabilities: Incorporate gender-sensitive approaches into food security assessments to account for reporting biases and gender-specific economic vulnerabilities, particularly in income-based indicators.
		\item Further testing, refining and optimizing the shortened modules for F2F Surveys particularly the expenditure module: Based on the findings that shorter modules yield similar results like the standard long ones, implementing shorter versions in face-to-face surveys to reduce respondent and enumerator fatigue (survey duration) as well as overall survey cost, without compromising data quality should be considered.
		\item Enhance enumerator training: Improving training for enumerators will to mitigate challenges associated with remote data collection, especially in populations where education is low.
		
	\end{itemize}

	\section{Supplementary Analysis}
	
	
	The livelihood distribution between the two modalities showed a sizeable imbalance, with respondents reporting small scale farming as their main source of livelihood representing over 50\% of the F2F participants and only 2\% of the remote households. Given that small farmers are generally among the most food insecure and by extension the most vulnerable households, this imbalance is likely to cause confounding and complicate the ability to assess the role of modality through direct analysis. Additionally, there is a very sparse representation of non-agricultural livelihoods among both the F2F and remote participants. Therefore, to compare results for the various assignment groups, a subgroup of 1,397 households representing 589 (32\%) of the F2F households and 808 (80\%) of the remote households with primary income derived agricultural production has been analysed. This subset is analysed to compared response patterns for each component of CARI/rCARI to determine the extent to which modality contributes to differences in food security classifications.

	%The average computed food expenditure share was significantly lower for the respondents assigned to the remote-short modality, with an average of 64\% compared with 68\% for the F2F-short group and 71\% for the F2F-long and remote-long” categories. However, it appears that 60 of the 417 respondents randomized to the remote-short group were assigned an FES score of 0, representing 14\% of the sample (compared with only two of the 589 F2F respondents and 26 of the 391 remote-long respondents). In such cases, the enumerator did not actually complete the FES module but the respondents were still assigned a score of 0 rather than a missing value. After correcting this error, both the “Remote/Long” and “Remote/Short” groups now had higher overall averages than the F2F groups, with averages of 76\% and 75\% respectively compared to 71\% and 69\% for the “F2F/Long” and “F2F/Short” groups. However, as for FCS, a large proportion of the variability in the responses (19\%) was due to geographic and enumerator variation, thus we fit a model including random effects for Enumerator ID and ADMIN5. In this case, there was still evidence of a 5\% increase in mean FES for the “Remote/Long” group relative to the F2F-long group and a 4\% increase in FES for the remote-short group, although the latter was not statistically significant. Controlling for respondent age and gender to the model indicated that FES scores for male respondents were estimated to be 3\% lower overall, while respondent age in general had no significant impact on scores. However, scores for households assigned to the remote-short group were significantly associated with respondent age, with an estimated increase of 2\% for every 10 years. Once respondent sex and the interaction between age and modality was included in the model, there were no longer significant differences due to modality alone. While these results are somewhat difficult to interpret and warrant additional investigation, they suggest that the increase in FES scores for remote respondents could be legitimately associated with demographic differences and not due to modality effects.

There was a highly significant difference in the proportion of households indicating that they had no food in the house among remote participants (40\% vs. 25\% for F2F respondents). While there is some evidence that factors such as enumerator sex and respondent age are significant factors in whether respondents report hunger, this is clearly an area that warrants further investigation as it cannot be explained only through the previously noted demographic differences between the remote and F2F participants in this subgroup.

Although the unadjusted data indicated that F2F respondents were far less likely to report a stable income relative to remote respondents (48\% vs 83\%), a full 46\% of the variation in responses can be explained by enumerator variation, and once random effects for these factors were added there were no longer significant differences associated with modality. This is concerning, as it indicates that the CARI Income indicator is highly dependent on enumerator bias. To illustrate, one remote enumerator indicated “No Income Change” for all 238 respondents they surveyed, which is extremely implausible given the high level of economic instability among these household.

	\subsection{Study limitations \& implementation challenges}
	
	Though the study has been implemented with utmost regard for all theoretical and practical guidelines and protocols underpinning experimental survey designs, the study nonetheless recorded the low response for the remote sample in comparison to the F2F sample. This affected the overall final sample distribution at ADMIN5 level. However, it must be mentioned that in spite of the high iteration in the remote sample, the final sample still exhibited a high level of statistical comparability based on their observable characteristics as baseline.
	
	
	Similarly, although extensive efforts were made to reduce and manage implementation challenges, some remained and affected the survey's successful implementation. These include:
	
	\begin{itemize}[label=--, left=2.5em]
		\item[1.] Restricted access to the field because of security concerns.
		\item[2.] Poor mobile phone network/infrastructure 
		\begin{itemize}[label=--, left=1.75em]
			\item[a.]	Poor network connectivity especially in the rural parts of the country affecting the response rate.
			\item[b.]	Bad infrastructure such as bad roads and frequent power cuts affected affecting the inclusion of the hard to reach areas of the country in the study.
		\end{itemize}
		\item[3.] Weather disruptions/rainfall causing floods disrupting the road network to the selected villages. Also, most of the potential respondents were away for their farm work affecting overall response rate.
	\end{itemize}
