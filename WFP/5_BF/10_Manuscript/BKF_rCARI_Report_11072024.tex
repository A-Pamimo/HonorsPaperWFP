	\textbf{Executive Summary}
	
	The remote Consolidated Approach for Reporting Food Insecurity (rCARI) is developed as an adaptation of the standard/conventional CARI methodology to assess food insecurity using remote survey data. The primary difference between rCARI and the standard CARI lies in the economic vulnerability indicator: the standard CARI uses Food Expenditure Share (FES) or Economic Capacity to Meet Essential Needs (ECMEN) while rCARI uses household’s income source and income change due to the challenges of collecting expenditure data remotely over the phone. Though rCARI is currently being piloted for assessments, the methodology has not been empirically validated. The objective of this study is to validate the rCARI methodology against CARI within the same sample populations.

	This study employs a survey experiment using a stratified sample to evaluate and validate the rCARI methodology against the standard CARI methodology. The sampling frame comprised the population from  three purposively selected regions—Plateau Central, Centro-Nord, and Nord Regions (ADMIN1). These regions were chosen based on mobile phone coverage, food security status, and accessibility, considering on-ground security challenges. The study sample was randomly selected from a full household census across 38 villages (ADMIN5) or primary sampling units (PSUs). In total, 4,500 households were drawn randomly (using the random number approach) from the census list of 10,554 households. 

	Sampled households were then randomly assigned to one of two study groups (survey modalities):
	\begin{itemize}
		\item [1.] \textbf{Face-to-Face (F2F) interviews:} Households received standard CARI modules.
		\item [2.] \textbf{Remote (phone) interviews:} Households received rCARI modules.
	\end{itemize}

	In Burkina Faso, challenges such as poor infrastructure and limited accessibility affected field operations. For instance, two ADMIN5 areas were excluded from the study due to security and inaccessible road network. The study ultimately interviewed 2,882 households out of a potential 4,256 households, with 1,863 (65\%) interviewed F2F and 1,019 (35\%) interviewed remotely.

	This report presents the preliminary analysis of data collected between 1–30 September 2024 in Burkina Faso and follows these steps:

	\begin{itemize}
		\item\textbf{Comparison of main food security indicators:} The main food security indicators (Food Consumption Score (FCS), Food Expenditure Score (FES), Reduced Coping Strategies Index (rCSI) and Livelihood Coping Strategies Index (LCSI)) are compared across F2F and remote modalities.
		\item\textbf{Calculation and validation of CARI Versions:}Three variations of CARI are calculated and compared against the standard CARI:
		\begin{itemize}
			\item[1.] CARI with Shortened FES that is collected in the remote modality.
			\item[2.] CARI with income source \& income change (F2F version).
			\item[3.] CARI with income source \& income change (remote version): This version was developed during the COVID-19 pandemic and is referred to as the standard rCARI in this report.
		\end{itemize}
	\end{itemize}

		
	The results highlight the following key findings:
	 
	\begin{itemize}
		\item In estimating the prevalence of acute food insecurity (AFI), CARI calculated using remote modules with the income source and income change indicator reported a significantly lower prevalence (approximately 15 percentage points) compared to standard CARI results from F2F modules. This difference was significant across all four CARI categories.
		\item Replacing the income source and income change indicator with the piloted shortened expenditure module implemented via phone resulted in no significant difference from standard F2F CARI in classifying households as acute food insecure (which is the combination moderately food insecure and severely food insecure categories).
		\item The survey modality had no significant effect on the performance of the income source and income change indicator, which was consistent across both F2F and remote modalities.
		\item In terms of the bias of the respondent when reporting their economic vulnerability, the results show that respondents are more likely to underestimate their economic vulnerability using the income source and income change indicator by between 14 to 17 percentage points compared to the FES regardless of survey modality.
		\item Comparisons of individual food insecurity indicators revealed that respondents interviewed over the phone generally reported better outcomes compared to F2F interviews.
		\item Regression analysis, controlling for baseline respondent characteristics, showed no statistically significant effect of survey modality on food insecurity group (being classied as food insecure, moderately food insecure and severely food insecure combined), regardless of whether FES or the income source and income change indicator was used. However, the gender of the respondent was significantly associated with the probability of the household being food insecure across both methods (CARI-F2F and rCARI).
		\item Respondent gender effects were more pronounced when using the income indicator, suggesting potential gender-specific income vulnerabilities or reporting biases that is not captured by FES-based classifications.
	\end{itemize}
	
		The overarching communication points from the foregoing is that:
	\begin{itemize}[left=2.5em]
		\item \textbf{Survey Modality (F2F vs. Remote):} While challenges such as connectivity and lower response rates affect remote surveys, when properly implemented can produce food security assessments that are up to 60\% - 70\% comparable to F2F surveys results.
		\item \textbf{Alignment of CARI and rCARI Methodologies:} CARI using the income source and income change indicator tends to report better food security outcomes regardless of the survey modality compared to the FES indicator.
	\end{itemize}

	\section{Introduction}
	
	Food security assessment and reporting are at the core of WFP’s mandate of addressing hunger and food insecurity. Over time, different methodologies (e.g, IPC, CARI, Cadre Harmonise, etc.) have been developed and used to assess and report global food insecurity each differing in scope, and purpose. A commonality that cuts across all methods is the use of primary household data collected through face-to-face interviews/surveys. However, in recent times particularly with the outbreak of the Covid-19 pandemic and attendant restrictions on movement and physical contact, data collection through conventional face-to-face method has been adversely affected. This negatively impacted the ability to assess food security needs because of the data gap created. Developing methodologies to accurately measure and interpret food insecurity especially in emergencies where physical access is restricted thus, is of great importance for WFP and many stakeholders. This is particularly important during emergencies when rapid assessment is required for advocacy and policy to save lives and livelihoods but physical access to the field is restricted either because of health threats or security considerations for enumerators. 
	
	To leverage the increased penetration of technology in operational areas of WFP during the Covid lockdowns, a remote version of the Consolidated Approach for Reporting Food Insecurity (CARI)- the so-called rCARI- was developed as an adaptation of the standard CARI methodology and administered remotely (over the phone). The methodology provided a stop-gap solution during the lockdown, however, questions about the validity of food security numbers it produces remain. For example, how are the remote modules comparable with already standardized face-to-face modules in capturing food insecurity. Although there exist validated studies on the use of face-to-face modules in assessing and reporting on food insecurity, remote methodology such as rCARI remains scant until now. 
	
	The potential advantages of the methodology abound for humanitarian work particularly in hard-to-reach areas where F2F assessment is practicably impossible. For example, the remote methodology has potential to save lives by providing real-time food insecurity data on the exposed population within the shortest possible time to inform policy and advocacy purposes. Furthermore, it can result in better use of scarce resources to achieve high end results by rolling out few targeted modules to assess food insecurity of a larger population.  	
	
	The overall objective of this study is to assess the comparability of the remote consolidated approach for reporting food insecurity - rCARI modules with the conventional CARI modules for assessing and reporting food insecurity. Specifically, the following indicators; Food Consumption Score (FCS), reduced Coping Strategy Index (rCSI), Livelihood Coping Strategy Index (LCSI), Food Expenditure Share (FES), and income sources and income change have been compared over the two modalities. The Economic Capacity to Meet Essential Needs (ECMEN) indicator which is sometimes used to aggregate CARI will not be tested in this study, as it requires the standard long expenditure module which cannot be collected over phone. In addition, ECMEN calculation requires having an updated value of the minimum expenditure basket (MEB) of the study population, which is not available for the study areas. In its stead, a shortened version of the standard expenditure module was tested as potential way to be used to collect household expenditure remotely.
	
	It is important to clarify that the objective of this study is not to report food security at the national level or aim for external validity across the entire population of each pilot country. Instead, the primary goal is to assess the comparability of the two methodologies: i.e., the standard Consolidated Approach for Reporting Food Insecurity (CARI) with its remote version (rCARI) when deployed within the same population. By focusing on the same population within each pilot country, the study seeks to understand how the two methodologies perform under similar conditions and contexts. This approach allows for a direct comparison of the effectiveness and accuracy of the two methodologies in assessing food security indicators.
	
	While the findings of this study is intended to provide valuable insights into the relative performance of the two methodologies within the same population, these may not be representative of food security trends at the national level. Therefore, the conclusions drawn from this study should be interpreted within the context of the population under investigation and not generalized to the entire country. By emphasizing internal validity within the selected populations, this study aims to provide robust evidence to inform decision-making and improve food security assessments, particularly in the context of remote data collection especially when that is the only method for collecting assessment data. For example, other factors aside the modules should be considered while reading results of any remote survey.
	
	\begin{itemize}[label=--, left=1.5em]
		\item[1.]	Mobile phone ownership (mobile phone penetration rate in the study area) could likely exclude the most vulnerable population from the sample. 
		\item[2.]	Mobile phone network coverage: Even in countries with high penetration rates, network coverage could be a problem, especially when electricity cuts are frequent and local infrastructure, such as communication masts, are not evenly distributed (World Bank, 2020).
		\item[3.]	Response rate: It is well documented that response rate in phone surveys is usually lower compared to F2F surveys (L’Engle et al., 2018; World Bank, 2020). 
	\end{itemize}
	
	These factors may lead to skewed sample bias from phone surveys especially if the goal to report national representative results/outcomes. Nevertheless, there are several statistical weighting techniques that can adjust the results of phone surveys using baseline variables like using demographics characteristics of the most recent face-to-face surveys (Nagpal et al. 2021; Glazerman et al. 2020; Valliant et al. 2013; Elkasabi, 2015). This allows the results of such exercises to be computed and extrapolated to reflect some level of representativeness. 
	
	However, the current study may not be affected by these confounding issues since the main objective is not to report results at national level but to assess the effectiveness of remote modules to collect and report on food insecurity using a statistically comparable stratified sample. Thus, this current analysis does not employ weighting techniques to adjust for sampling skewness and response rate. Nonetheless, the study design allowed for controlling sample skewness due to mobile phone ownership, and other underlying background variables that could be associated with non-response rate over the phone such as age, education, gender of the household head/respondent.
	
	In the next chapter the study design and implementation is presented in detail.

	\section{Study design \& sample}
		
	The study was implemented as a survey experiment with stratified sample comparing the outcomes of two statistically comparable samples drawn from the same population. 
	
	Selection of study areas and participating households followed a stratified random sampling approach (stratified by the different Admin Areas-biggest to smallest but not representative at each admin area) based on 3-point criteria developed and agreed together with the WFP Burkina country office. The criteria include, \textbf{1. Mobile phone coverage, 2. Food security situation, \& 3. Accessibility (considering the security situation on the ground).} 
	
	The study design followed rigorous randomization procedures to assign potential study respondents to one of two study groups: F2F or remote. The systematic steps in selecting the different administrative areas and study households with annotated explanation is presented in figure 1. 
	
	\begin{figure}[H]
		\begin{center}
			\includegraphics[scale=.65]{2_figure/BF_Sampling.png}
		\end{center}
		\label{fig:Sample_Frame}
		\caption{rCARI Validation Study Sampling Frame}
	\end{figure}
	
	\begin{itemize}[label=--, left=1.5em]
		\item[1.] STEP 1 - Based on the inclusion criteria (phone coverage, food security situation and accessibility), three regions were purposively selected for the pilot in Burkina Faso (Plateau Central, Nord \& Centro-Nord Regions). 
		
		\item[2.] STEP 2 - A total of four Provinces (ADMIN2), Ganzourgou, Sanmatenga, Yatenga and Passore, were purposively selected from the regions selected in step 1. The Administrative structure of Burkina Faso allowed for the inclusion of Admin3 areas (communes), therefore four communes were further selected; 2 communes from the Nord region and one each from Centro-Nord and Plateau Central regions respectively. 
		
		\item[3.] STEP 3 - Selection of villages (ADMIN5): A list of villages in the selected communes (that met the inclusion criteria set out in STEP 1) were compiled at this stage to form the primary sampling units (PSUs). A total of 39 PSUs were initially selected (distributed uniformly across the 4 selected communes). The selection of the villages have been done taking into consideration variation in food insecurity. See annex for the full list of administrative areas selected, number of households listed and their phone ownership.
		
		\item[4.] STEP 4 - Sampling of households for the study: The sampling frame for study was all households in selected PSUs. A minimum of 100 households per PSU were randomly selected from the census list. The final listed households in this stage were used for the randomization process in the next step.
		
		\item[5.] STEP 5 - Random assignment of household (hh): Consented final sampled households in STEP 4 are randomly assigned into one of two survey modalities/groups; F2F (CARI group) and Remote (rCARI group). The experiment was designed such that the full sample was divided equally between the two modalities.
	
		All interviews were conducted within the same time frame to ensure households in the two modalities are reporting food security situation over the same window. 
	\end{itemize}

	\begin{itemize}
		\item Majority of households classified as food secure by CARI using the income indicator are overwhelmingly also classified as food secure (98\%) by the FES indicator, with only one household being classified as marginally food secure category by the FES indicator. This indicates a perfect alignment for the two indicators in identifying food secure households.
		
		\item Similarly, more than half of all households (62.50\%) classified to be marginally food secure using the income indicator are also classified by the FES indicator to be in the same category, i.e., marginally food secure category. However, a significant proportion of the remaining households  (34.64\%) have been classified as being food secure by the FES indicator indicating these household are in the upper boundary of the food security classification than they have been classified by the income indicator.

The remaining households (20.83\%) are misclassified into the moderately food insecure category by the FES indicator.

	The overarching conclusion from the analysis of Table 9 reveals a strong consistency observed between income choice and income change and FES based CARI classifications, particularly for severely and moderately food insecure households. This alignment though not perfect provides evidence to support the use of the income based CARI (rCARI) as an effectiveness methodology for identifying vulnerable populations and by extension food insecure populations. However, variations observed in marginally food secure households indicate a need for further exploration of the data to understand these households.

	\subsubsection{Food Consumption Score (FCS)}

The responses are aggregated, and weights applied to obtain an index which is then used to categorize the household into one of three groups of food consumption: food secure, borderline, and poor.

	\subsubsection{Reduced Coping Strategies Index (rCSI)}
	
	The reduced Coping Strategies index (rCSI) is used to assess the frequency of occurrence of increasingly severe coping strategies within a households’ due to lack of adequate food, i.e., the behaviours individuals engage in when they can not access enough food. It measures the frequency and severity of the food consumption related behaviours by households to cope with food shortage within a 7-day recall period. The indicator is comprised of 5 standard strategies framed around how and who consumes food in times of shortage. It serves as a proxy indicator for food access. The higher the composite score, the more coping reported, and therefore the more food insecure the household is.
	
	Table 12 presents the results of the rCSI module compared over the two samples and modalities. This means that households tend to report extensive use of negative coping strategies in the F2F survey than they do on the phone. The difference in the overall rCSI between the two modalities is about 3 points and is statistically significant.

It assesses the household's strategies to meet dietary needs when faced with economic or food-related challenges. LCSI strategies are typically categorized into three levels: stress, crisis, and emergency. For targeting and assistance, only the crisis and emergency categories are generally considered, as they classify households as food insecure.

In terms of crisis and emergency strategies, 28\% of F2F respondents adopted these strategies, compared to 19\% of remote respondents.

While the remote sample shows a higher proportion of households not having adopted any coping strategies, the differences in the adoption of crisis and emergency strategies between the two groups (28\% for F2F vs. 19\% for remote) suggest a trend that warrants further investigation. 	\subsubsection{Food Expenditure Share (FES)}
	
	The Food Expenditure Share (FES) is a key indicator of food security, reflecting the proportion of a household's total consumption expenditure spent on food. A higher FES suggests greater economic vulnerability, as a larger portion of household resources is dedicated to food, potentially limiting spending on other necessities.

	Table 15 summarizes the FES across two survey modalities. The comparison between the remote and F2F surveys reveals no significant differences in the aggregated FES between the two methods.
	
	The FES is typically broken down into four standard categories for analysis: less than 50\%, 50-75\%, 75\% or more, and a non-food expenditure category. When comparing these categories across survey methods, mixed results emerge. Notably, a significantly larger proportion of F2F respondents report food expenditure shares between 50\% and 75\% of total household spending. In contrast, the remote survey sample shows a different distribution, with a majority of respondents reporting either very low (less than 50\%) or very high (75\% or more) FES.

	\section{Regression analysis}
	
	In this section of the analysis, a simple linear regression analysis is conducted to determine the effect of survey modality and phone ownership in explaining the food security (defined as moderately and severely food insecure based on CARI) of the household controlling for individual and household level characteristics. The model estimated is specified as follows:
	
	\[Y_{i}=\beta M_{i}+ \gamma P_{i}+ \delta X_{i}+ \varepsilon_{i} \tag{1}\] 
	
	where \(Y_{i}\) is a binary indicator of the food security status of the \(i^{th}\) household taking the value of one if the respondent is food insecure (made of the moderately food insecure plus severely food insecure CARI categories) and zero otherwise (calculated independently for CARI-FES - Food Insecure FES, and CARI-Inc - Food Insecure Income, indicators respectively), \(X_{i}\) is a vector of respondent's and household level characteristics such as age of respondent, sex, education in years, household size, dependency ratio and presence of persons with disability in the household, \(M_{i}\) is a binary indicator \(M_{i}=0/1\) taking the value of 1 if the mode of interview was F2F and zero if  phone, \(P_{i}\) is phone ownership indicator of the respondent \(P_{i}=1/0\) takes the value of one if the respondent owned a phone zero otherwise and \(\varepsilon_{i}\) is the error term clustered at ADMIN5 level. \(\beta\) and \(\gamma\) are the coefficients of interest showing the effect of modality and phone ownership on the \(i^{th}\) household's food insecurity outcome respectively.
	
	Equation (1) is implemented as a simple logistic regression for the full sample stepwise in three different configuration of the model defined by the number covariates included in each configuration. The results of the analysis are presented in Table 17 below. In the first stage, a base model which includes only the survey modality and gender of the respondent (columns 1 \& 4). The second model includes the base model controlling for additional baseline household demographics collected in the census exercise (columns 2 \& 5). The third and final estimation is the second model plus an interaction term of the sex of the household head and sex of the respondent (columns 3 \& 6).

For example, female respondents are between 5–6 percentage points less likely to be classified as food insecure compared to male respondents, and this relationship is statistically significant (p < 0.05) when using the FES indicator while they are up to 8 percentage points less likely to report food insecurity compared to male respondents using the income source indicator, with a higher statistical significance (p < 0.01).
	
	This results suggest that female respondents were significantly less likely to be classified as food secure consistently across both CARI-Inc and CARI-FES methodologies compared to males. The inclusion of an interaction term between sex of the respondent and sex of the household did not show any statistical significance. This may reflect:
	\begin{itemize}
	\item Differences in how males and female respondents perceive or report income changes and food expenditure.
	\item Gender-specific income vulnerabilities that influence the perceived severity of food insecurity
	\end{itemize}

Similarly, variables related to structural poverty (e.g., impoverished roof or floor) exhibit small effects but are not statistically significant. These put together show that while structural and demographic variables may contribute to food security outcomes, their lack of statistical significance in this model suggests that the sex of the respondent is a strong predictors of food security in Burkina Faso using the FES and income source and income change-based CARI classifications.

	\section{Summary \& conclusions}
	
	In this pilot study, we examine the effect of survey modality (Face-to-Face [F2F] vs. remote) on food security assessment in Burkina Faso. The study employs a survey experiment using a stratified random sample from three regions representing varying degrees of food insecurity and livelihood diversity. Households were randomly assigned to one of the two modalities based on their baseline observable characteristics which was collected during a household census phase. The two samples (F2F vs. remote) were then tested for statistical comparability of their baseline observable characteristics (statistical balance between the groups) as a pre-requisite for the implementation of the data collection.

	A total of 2,882 households were interviewed (F2F and remote altogether), using identical food security assessment modules. Data collection was carried out over a four-week period, with both modalities implemented simultaneously by trained and experienced enumerators. 
	
	The data provide important insights that should be considered in conducting food security assessment using remote methods (phone survey). Below are some key highlights from the analysis of the data from Burkina Faso pilot study.
	
	\begin{itemize}[left=2.5em]
		\item Respondents generally tend to overreport their food security outcomes indicators over the phone compared to F2F interviews. 
		\item The shortened FES module piloted remotely tend to generally produce results that are statistically comparable to the results of the standard FES implemented in F2F. 
		\item CARI\_Income: Household income as an indicator may be effective for capturing short-term economic shocks and acute vulnerabilities that do not necessarily reflect the long term consumption patterns and therefore especially valuable for assessment during crises like natural disasters or economic downturns, where income changes directly impact food security.
		\item Further analysis is needed to understand the dynamics of marginally and moderately food-secure households, where the CARI classification are significantly different for the two aggregation methods, i.e., CARI\_FES and CARI\_Income.
		\item The analysis of the long- and short- LCSI, FES and FCS respondents generally tend to report better off situation - slightly better food consumption, modules (F2F) overall does not show any statistically significant difference. This means that by fine-tuning and increased training, the short modules could potentially replace the longer versions in F2F surveys to reduce survey fatigue both for the interviewee and interviewer.
		\item In terms of the food security indicators, households tend to reports slightly higher food consumption (FCS) and better livelihood coping situations over the phone (remote modality) than when interviewed F2F. 
		%\item CARI\_FES: This probably best suited for long-term monitoring and identifying food insecurity prevalence. Thus, programs focusing on sustained support, such as livelihood improvement and food assistance, should rely on FES data.
	\end{itemize}
	
	The key communication points from the foregoing is that:
	\begin{itemize}[left=2.5em]
		\item \textbf{Survey Modality (F2F vs. Remote):} While challenges such as connectivity issues and lower response rates may not favor remote surveys, when properly implemented, remote surveys can produce food security assessments that are up to 60\% - 70\% comparable to F2F surveys results.
		\item \textbf{Alignment of CARI and rCARI Methodologies:} CARI using the income-based indicator as a measure of economic vulnerability of the population tends to adopt a conservative approach in estimating acute food insecurity compared to the use of the FES regardless of the survey modality.
		\item \textbf{Gender \& Income Indicator:} The sensitivity of the gender of respondent and the income choice and income change indicator highlight the importance of adopting nuanced and context-specific approaches to food security measurement.
	\end{itemize}
	
	
	\subsection{Recommendations}
	Based on the foregoing findings from the Burkina Faso data, the following recommendations are deduced:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Integrate and complement both Food Expenditure Share (FES) and the Income Choice \& Income Change indicators in reporting CARI (CARI-FES and CARI-Inc): The use of both FES and the income source and income change indicators in tandem will capture both long-term trends and short-term vulnerabilities in food security assessments, particularly during crises.
		\item Address gender-specific vulnerabilities: Incorporate gender-sensitive approaches into food security assessments to account for reporting biases and gender-specific economic vulnerabilities, particularly in income-based indicators.
		\item Further testing, refining and optimizing the shortened modules for F2F Surveys particularly the expenditure module: Based on the findings that shorter modules yield similar results like the standard long ones, implementing shorter versions in face-to-face surveys to reduce respondent and enumerator fatigue (survey duration) as well as overall survey cost, without compromising data quality should be considered.
		\item Enhance enumerator training: Improving training for enumerators will to mitigate challenges associated with remote data collection, especially in populations where education is low.
		
	\end{itemize}

	\subsection{Study limitations \& implementation challenges}
	
	Though the study has been implemented with utmost regard for all theoretical and practical guidelines and protocols underpinning experimental survey designs, the study nonetheless recorded the low response for the remote sample in comparison to the F2F sample. This affected the overall final sample distribution at ADMIN5 level. However, it must be mentioned that in spite of the high iteration in the remote sample, the final sample still exhibited a high level of statistical comparability based on their observable characteristics as baseline.
	
	
	Similarly, although extensive efforts were made to reduce and manage implementation challenges, some remained and affected the survey's successful implementation. These include:
	
	\begin{itemize}[label=--, left=2.5em]
		\item[1.] Restricted access to the field because of security concerns.
		\item[2.] Poor mobile phone network/infrastructure 
		\begin{itemize}[label=--, left=1.75em]
			\item[a.]	Poor network connectivity especially in the rural parts of the country affecting the response rate.
			\item[b.]	Bad infrastructure such as bad roads and frequent power cuts affected affecting the inclusion of the hard to reach areas of the country in the study.
		\end{itemize}
		\item[3.] Weather disruptions/rainy disrupting the road network to the selected villages. Also, most of the households were away for their farm work affecting overall response rate.
	\end{itemize}
