	\section{Introduction}
	The overall goal of this study was to assess the validity of the rCARI module(s) against conventional CARI modules for measuring food security within the same population. The study compares indicators collected through phone surveys with conventional face-to-face survey, considering the latter as the standard standard methodology/gold standard. The following food security indicators were compared: Food Consumption Score (FCS), reduced Coping Strategy Index (rCSI), Livelihood Coping Strategy (LCS), Food Expenditure Share (FES), and income sources and income change. The Economic Capacity to Meet Essetial Needs (ECMEN) indicator which is sometimes used to aggregate CARI will not be tested in this study, as it requires a standard long expenditure module which cannot be collected over the phone. In addition, ECMEN calculation requires having an updated value of the minimum expenditure basket (MEB) of the study population, which is not available for the study areas. In its stead, a shortened version of the standard expenditure module will be used to test the possibility of collecting FES data over the phone.
	
	Studying the validity of using the phone to collect food security indicators is a key step in judging the appropriateness of the rCARI methodology to report food insecurity prevalence. However, Testing the comparability of these indicators through different modalities of data collection (Face-to-Face (F2F) vs. Phone) should be read within the wider consideration around phone survey modality of data collection. For example, other factors aside the modules should be considered while reading results of any remote survey.
	
	\begin{itemize}[label=--, left=1.5em]
		\item[1]	First, mobile phone ownership (mobile phone penetration rate in the study area) could likely exclude the most vulnerable population from the sample. 
		
		\item[2]	Second, mobile phone network coverage; Even in countries with high penetration rates, network coverage could be a problem, especially when electricity cuts are frequent and local infrastructure, such as communication masts, are not evenly distributed (World Bank, 2020).
		
		\item[3]	Third, response rate: It is well documented that the response rate in phone surveys is usually lower than in face-to-face surveys (L’Engle et al., 2018; World Bank, 2020). Response rate is usually lower among the well-off population which could contribute to sample skewness.
	\end{itemize}
	
	These factors may lead to skewed sample from phone surveys especially if the goal to report national representative results/outcomes. Nevertheless, there are several statistical weighting techniques that can adjust the results of phone surveys based baseline variables like using demographics characteristics of the most recent face-to-face surveys (Nagpal et al. 2021; Glazerman et al. 2020; Valliant et al. 2013; Elkasabi, 2015). This allows the final results of such exercises to be aggregated and extrapolated to reflect some level of representativeness. However, this current study remains insulated from these confounding issues since the main idea is not to report results at the national level but rather to compare how the two methods perform within the same population with similar characteristics. For this reason, the study does not focus on weighting techniques to adjust for sampling skewness and response rate. Nonetheless, the study design adopted in Central African Republic (CAR), could allow for testing of sample skewness due to mobile phone ownership and coverage (see CAR sample design in Annex III). CAR has one of the lowest mobile phone penetration rates globally (approximately 70\% of the population do not own/use mobile phones).

	\section{Study Design}
	
	The study was implemented in a randomized controlled setup following and adhering to all standards and requirements of a Randomized Control Trial (RCT). However, for the current study, the methodology shall referred to as a randomized control study not confuse readers with the standard RCT as used in the medical literature. 
	
	Food security data was collected from two statistically comparable groups of households within the same sample. Standardized remote and face-to-face questionnaires were administered to the sampled households randomized to receive either of two modalities: remote or face-to-face. 
	
	
	Data collection was implemented from July 2023 to August 2023.
	
	\subsection{Study are and sample selection}
	
	Selection of study areas and participating households/respondents followed a stratified random (stratified by the different Admin Areas-biggest to smallest) sampling approach selection based on 3-point criteria developed and agreed together with the country office in Bangui. 
	
	\begin{itemize}
	\item[1.] Mobile phone coverage.
	\item[2.] Food security situation.
	\item[3.] Accessibility (considering the security situation on the ground).
	\end{itemize}
	
	Two Administration 1 (ADMIN1) Areas (Prefecture) were selected based on the afore mentioned criteria. 
	\begin{itemize}
	\item[1.] Nana- Gribizi - Western part of the country and,
	\item[2.] Mbomou - Eastern part of the country.
	\end{itemize}

	Selection of Admin2, 3 and 5 were randomly done based on a compiled list of areas that had mobile phone connectivity - a full list of areas with phone network was compiled through Orange/Moove/Telecel. The biggest mobile phone operators in the country with most phone coverage. Only Admin 3 \& 5 areas with phone coverage were then included in the sampling frame at this stage to ensure that sampled households could be interviewed remotely when assigned to the remote module. 
	
	In total, 2 Admin2 areas (sub-prefecture) one each from Admin1 area and a total of 4 Admin3 areas selected randomly from the two Admin2 areas. The final list of Admin Areas selected for the study are:
	
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Admin2 Areas (Sub-Prefecture) selected
			\item[1.] Bangassou
			\item[2.] Kanga-Bandaro
	\end{itemize}
	
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Admin3 Areas selected
			\item[1.] Bangassou
			\item[2.] Nana
			\item[3.] Botto
			\item[4.] Kanga-Bandaro 
	\end{itemize}
